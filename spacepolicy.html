The History of United States Spaceflight

1950s-present

1950

The Beginning of the Space Race

The Launch of Sputnik 1

October 4, 1957

The Soviet Union surprises the world and plunges Americans into paranoia as the world’s first artificial satellite orbits the Earth. Traveling at 29,000 kilometers per hour and traveling a total of 70 million kilometers, Sputnik represented the beginning of a new era for American scientific policy and ignited a race between two global superpowers that united the country in an effort to reach the stars.

National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958

July 29, 1958

The National Aeronautics and Space Act enacted during the Eisenhower presidency created the administration bearing the same name in response to incapability of solely the United States military to keep up with the space race against the Soviets. The newly formed agency aimed to preserve the role of the United States as a leader in aeronautical and space science and technology, to develop and operate vehicles capable of carrying instruments, equipment, supplies and living organisms through space, and to expand human knowledge of phenomena in the atmosphere and space.

1960

"We choose to go to the Moon"

President Kennedy's Call to Action

September 12, 1962

Following a series of triumphs from the Soviet Union’s space program, then President John F. Kennedy gave a speech in an attempt to persuade the American public to support the national effort to land a man on the Moon and return him safely to Earth. This specific goal and direction united the country to put forth the best and the brightest in order to achieve this goal. From coast to coast, Americans took pride in their efforts to bring a man to the Moon by the end of the decade, and it was ultimately this dedication that allowed for its successful fruition.

"One small step for man, one giant leap for mankind."

July 21, 1969

On July 21, 1969 at 2:56 UTC, Neil Armstrong became the first human to step onto another celestial body, the Moon. This achievement was a result of the culmination of years of work from American scientists and engineers and effectively ended the space race. Apollo 11 was the first of many successful missions to the Moon and a milestone for manned spaceflight.

1970

Testing the Boundaries of Innovation

Skylab

May 14, 1973

The United States began to solidify its place amongst the stars when it launched its first space station in 1973. Weighing over 170,000 pounds, it was placed into a low Earth orbit where it was the home to many experiments especially in the realm of crew survival and solar observations. This project is known as the precursor to the International Space Station. Following six years in orbit, it re-entered the atmosphere in a spectacular fashion.

Launch of the Voyager Program

August 20 & September 5, 1958

Voyager 1 and 2 were both launched in 1977 with the intent to flyby the outer planets of the Solar System and following the completion of their respective objectives, both probes continued flying through space eventually reaching interstellar space. To date, Voyager 1 has travelled over 20 billion miles and is now the farthest spacecraft from Earth. Despite this extreme distance, both Voyager 1 and 2 regularly communicate with NASA and are expected to remain operational until 2025.

1980

The Space Shuttle Era

Launch of the Space Shuttle

April 12, 1981

The United States continued on its promise to remain the leader in spaceflight as it introduced a reusable low Earth orbital spacecraft system that would later become a recognizable flagship for the American space program. Six total orbiters were constructed following the loss of the Challenger in 1986 and Columbia in 2003. The remaining orbiters include Enterprise, Discovery, Atlantis, and Endeavour. The Space Shuttle continued to be the American space travel workhorse until the program’s conclusion in 2011.

Test

Galileo probe

October 18, 1989

Galileo was an unmanned space probe launched at the end of the decade which aimed to study Jupiter and its moons. It was launched via Space Shuttle Atlantis and arrived within Jupiter’s vicinity 6 years ilater in December 1995. This probe observed the magnetosphere, atmospheric conditions of Jupiter and the plasma interactions from Io’s volcanism and Jupiter’s atmosphere.

1990

A Home Among the Stars

International Space Station

1998

The International Space Station (ISS) had its first component launched into orbit in 1998 and was under construction until 2011 when the final main component (Leonard) was delivered and attached to the station. It is the product of an international coalition who aim to use the station as a space environment research laboratory that conducts experiments in the fields of (human) biology. physics, astronomy, meteorology among others. The ISS is scheduled to continue operating until 2024.

Test

Hubble Space Telescope

May 20, 1990

The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is one of the largest and most versatile space telescope. Originally launched in 1990, the telescope didn’t reach its full capability until 1993 when corrective optics were installed to address blurry images as a result of a incorrectly shaped mirror. The HST has been credited with providing the most detailed visible light images, and providing a deep view into space which have led to breakthroughs in astrophysics such as determining the rate of expansion of the Universe.

2000

New Challenges for a New Century

End of Space Shuttle Era

July 8, 2011

The era of the Space Shuttle came to an end in 2011 with the final flight STS-135 with Atlantis. At the conclusion of the mission, all the remaining orbiters were distributed across the United States for preservation in various museums. To date, NASA is currently in the process of developing a new launch system that will once again be able to transport astronauts to the International Space Station and beyond.

Test

Rise of Commercialized Spaceflight

The new century has also allowed for the private enterprises to enter the realm of spaceflight. SpaceX and Blue Origin are seen as the two main players in filling the gap left behind by NASA’s conclusion of the Space Shuttle program. The emergence of privatized flight ushers in a new era of innovation including reusability, next generation construction materials and the potential for manned spaceflight beyond the Moon.
